# Sincline Biomedical
## Overview
Sincline Biomedical is the largest military contractor in the field of wetware augments — brain-device interfaces implanted directly onto the human brain that let the wearer control nearby technology with thought alone. The augments are available commercially, and militaries across the globe outfit their soldiers with Sincline's latest wetware.

The company has a reputation for recklessness and arrogance, but it's also enormous, well-connected, and — by all appearances — fully above-board, complete with major government contracts, regulatory oversight, a well-staffed compliance department, and some of the best PR known to man.

## History
Sincline broke into the market with small implanted communication devices that interfaced directly with the brain, letting team members exchange data silently over long distances. Militaries found this invaluable for commanding soldiers in the field.

As combat drones and other battlefield technology evolved, Sincline engineered implants that could connect to nearby devices remotely — and the brain-device interface was born. From there, the company began experimenting with offloading certain mental processes onto the implants themselves, granting soldiers superhuman reflexes and heightened agility.

As far as the public is aware, that's the extent of what present-day wetware can do. Sincline recently announced that it's developing augments capable of moving a wearer's limb automatically — out of the path of an oncoming bullet, for example. This is considered the cutting edge of what the company can achieve.

Behind the scenes, Sincline has been able to control limbs through wetware for some time now, including completely remotely. Their labs have gone as far as creating implants that allow a near-complete override of the wearer's body — capabilities they've kept carefully out of the public eye.

## Human Experimentation
Sincline routinely recruits soldiers to volunteer for its newest wetware trials, promising generous compensation and support for them and their families. Volunteers are told that their efforts will improve everyone's lives. These recruitments happen in full public view, and as far as anyone's concerned, these willing participants are being treated with the utmost care and professionalism. Occasionally the company recruits civilians as well, to ensure its augments work on the general population.

Because the implants are partly biological in nature, it's assumed they don't need much maintenance — though they haven't been around long enough for anyone to know for sure. Early research suggests that removing an implant becomes more difficult the longer it remains attached to the wearer, but that research in this area is only now getting off the ground.
