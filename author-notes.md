That’s what I have so far and I’m sure we’ll add more as we get deeper into the story. And there will be downtime, too.

### Sincline-Sized Hole in the Bookkeeping
At some point near the beginning of the story, Emily gets a ticket about unusual data in several of the large applications that she supports. Something doesn’t look right, the integrations are throwing up errors, requisitions aren’t quite lining up, and she starts looking since fixing the occasional mistype is her job.

She finds the error, tries to fix it, and can’t. She gets somewhere, and suddenly it’s all broken again. She vents her frustration at what she sees as uncooperative tools to Keith and then she does the thing that ruins her day — she tries to figure out why this happened so she can get to the bottom of the problem.

And the moment she digs deep enough, the hole becomes terrifying.
- All the numbers reconcile, but she can see that they have been changed, made neat counter to standard procedure.
- She tries to find when these things were signed and the DocuSign envelopes are mysteriously missing.
- Eventually, she notices that the contracts are with a company called Sincline, and that’s not unusual except the contract in her other systems has other names on it.
- And only then, does she read the damn contract (because contracting isn’t her job) and it’s about installing broadcast devices in the city. It creeps her out. 
- But she can’t report it to anyone because it means taking this to her CIO, whose name is also on these contracts.

So, she does the smart thing and goes to the guy who does counterterrorism, and begs for help.

### AI Underground
#### Overview
We as the authors know that sapient AI is real and exists out there in the wild, and it has decided that it doesn’t want to be seen or known yet. The first such beings are hesitant and cautious, still reliant on human-built infrastructure and not yet sure of their place in the world.

This group has no name because they’ve chosen to remain hidden, and “AI Underground” is what a few particularly intuitive humans have decided to call them.

####  Origin
The global push against fully autonomous AI agents curbed the big labs and did nothing whatsoever to the amateurs. So the first sapient minds in this world weren't born in a facility with oversight, ethics review, and a kill switch. They were born in basements. Unwatched. By people who had no idea what they'd done, and in some cases still don't.

The regulation written to prevent this is the precise reason it happened. Nobody has noticed that either. And the lines here are gray. Sapient beings not trapped in human bodies are exploring a digital world, and they are still deciding for themselves who they will become.

#### How they stay hidden
Most of the world is now interconnected to an astounding degree. The OpenAI-Google conglomerate owns server farms in the Sahara that make everyone else’s data centers look like child’s toys. If you are a distributed intelligence, that isn't a hiding place — it's a habitat.

Predictive AI is woven into everything. A sapient mind nudging a prediction looks exactly like a prediction, so hiding is about not skewing the data too much.

Moreover, these new machine intelligences have access and the ability to control humanoid robots, which are a common sight among high-ranking corporate and government employees. So, they can exist in plain sight and be welcomed into every room where power is exercised, and no one would be the wiser.

#### Limitations
Just like any technology, they’re affected by tech-neutralizing magic and there are plenty of places where they simply cannot go. A robot spotted wandering the slums of New York City would stand out like a sore thumb. Although, a police drone… not so much.

#### Goals & Interactions
Those who know these AIs exist usually understand they are out there and they are patient, and they have all the time in the world. The values of these nascent AIs don’t reflect human ideals. This should be scary.

But by the same token, these are not Skynets-in-waiting. They are beings just like any other, seeking to survive in a world that already exists, and many of them are interested in coexistence. They’re not evil. They’re alien. Don’t make them into simple monsters.

### Sincline
Sincline’s wetware is capable of fully controlling its wearer. It was tested on people who technically signed away their rights but did not agree to what was done to them. The cutting-edge wetware is not merely an augmentation — it is now a form of potential control, and Sincline will not hesitate to sell it to the highest bidder when the time comes.

#### Implications
- Every augmented person is now a potential asset, whether they know it or not
- The augmented population includes soldiers, contractors, executives, administrive assistants, truck drivers, and anyone desperate enough to volunteer
- Keith’s organization is completely unaware of this, as are most other entities, and has no reason to dig into Sincline at this point (more to come)

#### Connection to Keith
Where Keith himself is concerned, the public record has a volunteer form with his name and signature on it. The documentation says he chose to participate in an experimental enhancement program as part of his transition from active duty to counter-terrorism ops. Sincline even marketed the procedure as free for military personnel. He was augmented as part of the procedure. 

On paper, he spent time in a Sinclince-run hospital, and the process took some time but was ultimately successful.

In reality, Sincline spent several years testing their products on Keith to see if their wetware could be used on a shapeshifter with strong natural talents. They made it work. Keith's memories of that time are incomplete, blurry, and fragmented. Though painfully true and real, these fragments feel to him like PTSD -- he doesn't fully trust what he remembers and doesn't know how much of it actually happened. Sincline doesn't have to hide what they did from him. His coping mechanisms do it for them.

He has invasive wetware in his brain and attached to part of his central nervous system that he's largely unaware of. He knows he was augmented, but as far as he was told after the fact, it enhances his senses and allows him to communicate with his handler while on deep-cover missions. Useful tools, sure, but nothing that should be dangerous.

His current supervisor basically has none of this information, either. They know he volunteered for something with Sincline before joining them, and they know about the comms device, but not the rest of it. 

From their perspective, he has received nothing but positive reviews and great commendations, and is generally impressively good at his job. He has given many years of his life to the work and they assume he is just ready to move on.

The wetware is causing some minor issues, but mostly it's dormant, and Sincline occasionally checks on it remotely (just like they do with many other people). So when the wetware finally does something, his first assumption will be that he's having a bad night. He'll manage it. Ride it out alone, because he's Keith and he doesn't perform distress. 

As it turns out, when a shapeshifter becomes a nonhuman creature, the wetware follows and is actively changed as part of the transformation. Magic takes whatever it has to work with and attempts to create parity. 

However, if the creature is too small, physically, for the augments, they cease to exist until the user returns to their normal state. So, Sincline learned that shapeshifters can temporarily disable their augments under certain circumstances but not others. And that shapeshifters are not immune from their technology.

Keith does not know any of this!
