# Magic
## Mechanics
### Source
Magic is a normal and natural part of the world, akin to the weather and the water cycle. Some people are born with a talent for using magic in the same way some people are born left-handed. Someone with a talent for magic is called a mage, and mages have differing affinities and skill sets. Magic is often not even remotely flashy or obvious; many people go their whole lives never knowing they have any kind of magical ability.

Some people can shapeshift into animals, while others are drawn to fire or other parts of the natural world. Healers exist, for example, and can greatly speed up a body's natural abilities. There are speculations that, as technology becomes ubiquitous, some people will be born with a magical affinity towards tech.

### How casting works
Casting magic requires four distinct parts: talent, energy, willpower, and a definitive result that the caster can visualize. When a person casts, they often describe feeling like something inside them wakes and draws on their energy and emotions before spilling out of them to create some resulting effect.

Defined effects can be taught. Someone can learn to create a ward against technology -- words can be used to share this information. But the talent has to exist, at minimum, and the person's affinity has to be in line with the effect. That said, there are different ways of achieving the same outcome, so wards come in all shapes and sizes depending on who casts them and how.

It's as messy and fuzzy as mycelium networks.

### Cost
Ultimately, magic costs energy. It uses the energy of a person to make something happen, causing mages to become exhausted quicker than normal. Depending on the difficulty of the magic, a person might need a few hours or a few days to recover. There are no known long-term consequences to using magic (as far as we know).

### Limits & rules
Magic is limited mainly by how much energy any single person has. There have been tales of multiple people combining their skills to create something no single person would be able to, but that's mostly folk tales.

Magic also can't bring the dead back to life. That one has been known for a long time, and the assumption is that people can't have an affinity for death. But there are mages out there right now testing those waters.

### Rarity & who can use it
Magic is actually pretty common in this world, and one out of every five people has the talent. Most just don't know about their abilities, and there are no tests to find out if someone is a mage. Someone can discover their talent at any age, adding to the confusion.

The world at large believes that it's more like one person out of every 150 or so humans. And skills are varied to such an extent that you're not likely to meet someone with the same affinity as yourself at your local park and know about it.

It doesn't help that people are suspicious and hesitant around magic (because accidents can happen and can be deadly). So, it's not talked about and only taught in school as "if you feel this particular feeling, visit the school mage to discuss some light reading."

### Future
Just like humans learned to create complicated models of weather pattern prediction, there's a theory in this world that people will one day be able to use technology to model how magic works. It hasn't happened yet, but give it another century or two.

## Types
### Shapeshifting
Some people in this world are born with the ability to shapeshift into animals. It is an inborn trait, not a learned skill. A shapeshifter has at most one or two animal forms, fixed from birth. Forms are always animals that exist in this world — real, natural creatures. There are no mythical forms (no dragons, phoenixes, etc.). 

Shapeshifting is extremely energy intensive. This creates a natural limit on the ability: a shapeshifter can only hold an animal form for a limited time before exhaustion forces them back. After shifting, they need to rest and recover before they can shift again.
